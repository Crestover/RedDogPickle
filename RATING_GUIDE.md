# How Red Dog Ratings Work

A plain-English guide for group organizers and players.

---

## The Short Version

Every player in your group gets a **Red Dog Rating (RDR)** — a number that reflects how well they've been playing against the other people in your group. The more you play, the more accurate it gets. If you stop playing for a while, the system doesn't punish you — it just becomes less sure about where you stand until you come back.

---

## What Your Rating Actually Means

Your RDR starts at **1200**. Win games and it goes up. Lose games and it goes down. How much it moves depends on a few things:

- **Who you beat (or lost to).** Beating a higher-rated team moves your rating up more than beating a lower-rated team. Losing to a better team barely hurts you. Losing to a team you should have beaten hurts more.

- **How close the game was.** An 11-9 win moves the needle less than an 11-3 win. This prevents lucky squeakers from inflating ratings and recognizes dominant performances.

- **How confident the system is in your rating.** If you've played 50 games in the last month, the system is very confident about your number and won't move it much. If you just came back after three months off, it's less sure where you stand, so it'll adjust faster to figure out your current level.

None of this requires you to think about math. Just play games and the system handles the rest.

## The Tier Badges

Your rating maps to a tier label that shows up on leaderboards:

| Rating | Tier |
|--------|------|
| Below 1100 | Walk-On |
| 1100–1199 | Challenger |
| 1200–1299 | Contender |
| 1300–1399 | All-Star |
| 1400+ | Elite |

These are cosmetic — they give players a quick sense of where they stand without needing to compare exact numbers.

## The Confidence System

This is what makes Red Dog different from a basic rating.

Every player has a hidden **confidence level** that reflects how much the system trusts their current rating. You'll see this as a small label on the leaderboard:

- **Locked In** — You've been playing regularly. Your rating is stable and well-established.
- **Active** — You're playing often enough that your rating is reliable.
- **Rusty** — You haven't played in a few weeks. Your rating is still there, but the system is less certain it's accurate.
- **Returning** — You've been away for a while. Your rating will adjust more quickly over your next few games to recalibrate.

### The important thing: your rating never drops just because you stopped playing.

Some systems penalize inactivity by lowering your rating. Red Dog doesn't do that. If you were a 1350 when you stopped playing and you come back two months later, you're still a 1350. The system just becomes less certain that 1350 is still right, so it'll move your rating faster — up or down — based on how you actually perform when you return.

This matters because it's fair. Life gets in the way. People travel, get injured, take breaks. They shouldn't lose their hard-earned rating because of it. But the system also shouldn't pretend that a two-month-old rating is just as reliable as one from last week. Confidence handles that tension.

---

## How Returning Players Are Handled

When someone comes back after a long break (roughly two months or more), the system gives them a short adjustment window — about three games. During that window, their rating still moves, but the swings are slightly dampened so that one bad game after a long layoff doesn't cause a wild drop. By the third game back, the system treats them normally again.

Think of it like a warm-up period. The system is watching, adjusting, but not overreacting.

## Why Doubles Ratings Are Hard (and What We Do About It)

In singles, it's simple — you won or you lost, and the rating adjusts. Doubles is messier because your result depends partly on your partner.

Red Dog handles this in a few ways:

- **Team average matters.** The system looks at the average rating of each team, not individual ratings. If a strong player teams up with a weaker player, the expected outcome shifts accordingly.

- **Partner gap dampening.** When there's a big rating gap between partners, the system adjusts less aggressively. This prevents inflated swings from highly imbalanced pairings. A 1400 player paired with an 1100 player won't get the same rating boost as if they'd been paired with a 1300.

- **Volume smooths it out.** Over dozens of games with rotating partners, individual skill emerges clearly. One game might be noisy, but the pattern over 30 or 50 games is reliable.

This is an honest limitation of any doubles rating system. No system perfectly isolates individual skill in a team sport. But with enough games in a consistent group, the ratings become surprisingly accurate.

## Why This Works Especially Well for Established Groups

If your group plays regularly — the same 10 to 30 people showing up week after week — Red Dog's ratings become very meaningful, often more useful than a broader system for understanding skill within your group.

Here's why:

### Everyone is calibrated against each other

In a closed group, every game directly informs the system about how players compare to each other. After a few months of regular play, the ratings reflect real observed performance across many different partner combinations and matchups. There's no guesswork — the data comes from your actual games.

### The sample is clean

Broader rating systems face a challenge: the ratings of your opponents matter, and those opponents' ratings depend on *their* opponents, and so on. When someone brings a rating from a tournament in another city, you're trusting a chain of comparisons involving people nobody in your group has ever seen play. That rating might be accurate or it might not — there's no way to know.

In a consistent group, every rating in the system is grounded in games you've watched happen. The comparisons are direct.

### It reflects how you actually play together

Pickleball at the group level is different from tournament pickleball. Different pace, different communication styles, different levels of intensity. A player's skill within your regular group — where they know everyone's tendencies, where the games are social but competitive — is a real and useful thing to measure. That's what Red Dog captures.

### It's not trying to be something it isn't

Red Dog isn't claiming to produce a universal skill rating that transfers across all contexts. It's producing a rating that tells you, within your group, who's been playing well, who's improving, who's been away, and how competitive any given matchup is likely to be. That's what group organizers actually need when setting up games and balancing courts.

## Red Dog and DUPR

If you or your players use DUPR, that's great — keep using it. DUPR is designed to be a universal rating across all competitive play: tournaments, leagues, open play with strangers. That's a different and valuable goal.

Red Dog is designed for something more specific: **understanding skill dynamics within your group.** It doesn't replace DUPR any more than your group's internal win-loss record replaces ATP rankings. They measure different things at different scales.

Where Red Dog adds value:

- **No external dependencies.** You don't need to submit results anywhere, wait for results to process, or hope your opponents' profiles are accurate. Every game you record is reflected immediately.

- **Self-play counts.** Many groups play mostly among themselves. Those games are fully valid in Red Dog. You don't need outside opponents to calibrate the system.

- **It's tuned for your group's reality.** Partner rotation, skill gaps, irregular attendance, casual blowouts, competitive close games — the system handles all of it with context that a universal system can't have.

- **It's private.** Your group's ratings are your group's business. There's no public profile, no leaderboard that strangers can see, no pressure to protect a number.

Think of DUPR as your passport rating — it tells the world roughly where you stand. Think of Red Dog as your home court rating — it tells your group exactly where you stand with the people you actually play with.

## What Organizers Should Know

### Ratings need volume to be meaningful

After 5 games, ratings are rough estimates. After 20 games, they're starting to be useful. After 50+ games, they're reliable. Encourage regular play and the system takes care of itself.

### New players settle in quickly

A new player starts at 1200 with high uncertainty. The system adjusts their rating aggressively for their first several games, then stabilizes. By roughly 8 to 10 games, their rating reflects their actual level within the group.

### The GOAT badges are fun but earned

The highest-rated active player (with 20+ games in the Elite tier) gets a Reigning GOAT badge. The player with the highest peak rating ever (50+ games) gets an All-Time GOAT badge. These are conversation starters, not just vanity — they reflect sustained high performance.

### You don't need to explain the math

Players don't need to understand confidence scores or volatility multipliers. All they need to know is: "Play games and your rating gets more accurate. Take a break and it won't punish you, but it'll adjust faster when you come back." That's it.

## One-Sentence Summary

Red Dog Rating tracks how well you play within your group, rewards consistent performance, doesn't punish you for taking breaks, and gets smarter the more your group plays together.
